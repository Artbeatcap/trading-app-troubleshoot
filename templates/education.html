What Are Options?

Options are financial contracts that give the buyer the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset at a specified price (strike price) on or before a specified date (expiration date).

Key Components:
  • Call Options: Give you the right to buy the underlying asset
  • Put Options: Give you the right to sell the underlying asset
  • Strike Price: The price at which you can buy/sell the asset
  • Expiration Date: The last day you can exercise the option
  • Premium: The price you pay for the option contract

Understanding the Greeks

Delta

Measures how much the option price changes for every $1 move in the underlying stock.

  • Call options: 0 to +1
  • Put options: -1 to 0
  • ATM options: ~0.5 for calls, ~-0.5 for puts
Gamma

Measures how much Delta changes for every $1 move in the underlying stock.

  • Highest for ATM options
  • Decreases as options move ITM/OTM
  • Always positive for both calls and puts
Theta

Measures how much the option price decreases each day due to time decay.

  • Always negative (time decay)
  • Accelerates as expiration approaches
  • ATM options have highest theta
Vega

Measures how much the option price changes for every 1% change in implied volatility.

  • Always positive for both calls and puts
  • Highest for ATM options
  • Decreases as expiration approaches
Quick Tips
In-the-Money (ITM): Option has intrinsic value
At-the-Money (ATM): Strike price equals current stock price
Out-of-the-Money (OTM): Option has no intrinsic value

Popular Options Strategies

Long Call

Buy a call option expecting the stock to rise significantly.

  • Risk: Limited to premium paid
  • Reward: Unlimited upside
  • Best for: Bullish outlook with leverage
Long Put

Buy a put option expecting the stock to fall significantly.

  • Risk: Limited to premium paid
  • Reward: Limited to strike price
  • Best for: Bearish outlook with protection
Covered Call

Sell a call option against stock you own.

  • Risk: Limited upside potential
  • Reward: Premium income
  • Best for: Income generation
Iron Condor

Sell OTM put and call spreads simultaneously.

  • Risk: Limited and defined
  • Reward: Premium collected
  • Best for: Range-bound markets

Risk Management Principles

1. Position Sizing

Never risk more than 1-2% of your total capital on any single trade. This ensures you can survive a string of losses.

2. Stop Losses

Always have an exit plan. Set stop losses based on technical levels or percentage losses.

3. Diversification

Don't put all your eggs in one basket. Spread risk across different strategies and underlying assets.

4. Time Decay Awareness

Options lose value over time. Be mindful of expiration dates and theta decay.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Over-leveraging: Using too much margin or buying too many contracts
Ignoring Greeks: Not understanding how options behave
No Exit Plan: Not having clear profit targets or stop losses
Chasing Losses: Increasing position size after losses

Advanced Options Concepts

Implied Volatility

Implied volatility (IV) is the market's expectation of future volatility. It's a crucial component of option pricing.

  • High IV = Expensive options
  • Low IV = Cheap options
  • IV tends to revert to the mean
Volatility Skew

Different strike prices have different implied volatilities, creating a "skew" pattern.

  • Put skew: OTM puts more expensive
  • Call skew: OTM calls more expensive
  • Reflects market sentiment
